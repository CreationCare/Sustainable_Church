Formation

Formation: Toward a Theology of Creation

Goal: To move beyond the “climate silence” in our pews and reclaim our role as stewards of God’s handiwork.

1. Breaking the “Climate Silence”

There is a persistent myth that people in our pews aren’t concerned about our changing climate. However, data suggests we are suffering from a “perception gap” rather than a lack of concern.

  • The Reality: 72% of Americans are concerned about climate change.
  • The Perception: Most people believe only 52% of their neighbors care.
  • The Result: To avoid perceived conflict, we stop talking about it. This silence inadvertently feeds the myth that the majority doesn’t care, when the opposite is true. The reality is that “climate dismissives” are a small, albeit vocal, minority.

We strive to encourage loving and practical conversation about the challenges of climate change and environmental degradation, informed by accurate information and abundant civility.

2. The Role of the “Silent Believers”

Our clergy are often the most prepared to lead this conversation, yet the most hesitant to start it.

  • Broad Consensus: Nearly 90% of clergy acknowledge that climate change is real.
  • Human Agency: 60% believe humans play a significant role, while another 30% believe humans play a lesser but still present role.
  • The Discussion Gap: Despite these beliefs, only half of clergy have discussed the topic with their congregations, and only 25% have addressed it more than once or twice.

We strive to encourage our clergy to actively help create a space where faith and science meet in service to God’s Creation.

3. Formation Resources

We have curated a library of tools to help your congregation engage with Creation Theology at every level.

🏛️ Leadership & Clergy

  • Preaching Creation: Homiletic resources for integrating stewardship into the liturgical year.
  • Visionary Leadership: Templates for leading vestries and boards through “green” transitions.

👧 Children & Youth

  • God’s Gardeners: Sunday School curricula focused on the wonder of nature.
  • Eco-Justice for Teens: Discussion guides for connecting faith with environmental advocacy.

📚 Community Learning

  • Book Club Recommendations: View our curated list of books on theology, ecology, and hope—complete with reviews and discussion prompts.
  • Adult Formation Series: Multi-week curricula exploring the biblical foundations of “Creation Care.”

4. Gentle Introductions

Not every conversation needs to start in a classroom. Sometimes, the best theology is practiced with our hands in the dirt and our feet on the earth.

  • Stewardship of the Land: Resources for water-wise gardening, xeriscaping, and planting native species on church grounds and at home.
  • Plalking (Pick-up + Walking): A spiritual practice of “walking and picking up”—caring for the neighborhood while caring for the soul.
  • Sacred Excursions: Birding and nature walks designed as “contemplative excursions” to reconnect with the local environment.
