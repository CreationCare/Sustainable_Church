Wildfire and Smoke

🔥 Wildfire & Smoke: Creating a Defensible Sanctuary

Goal: To prevent building ignition from wind-blown embers and maintain “Clean Air” zones to protect vulnerable residents from hazardous smoke.

1. Foundational Resilience (One-Time Upgrades)

These “once-and-done” structural improvements significantly increase the chance of a building surviving a wildfire without active intervention.

  • Fire Department Audit: Invite your local fire department to conduct a professional wildfire risk audit. They can provide specific countermeasures tailored to your local terrain.
  • Roofing & Eaves: Evaluate roofing material to ensure it is non-combustible (e.g., metal, tile, or Class A asphalt). Ensure all eaves are “boxed” or closed to prevent embers from being trapped.
  • Siding & Vents: Replace combustible siding with fire-resistant materials (fiber cement, brick, stone). Cover all external vents with fine wire mesh.
  • Propane Safety: Ensure any propane tanks are located at least 15 feet away from any building structure.
  • Evacuation Planning: Develop a formal evacuation plan that includes primary and secondary routes for the congregation and any weekday programs (like daycares).

2. The Defensible Space System

Wildfire defense is about managing “fuel” in concentric circles around the church.

The 0–5 Foot “No-Burn Zone”

  • Remove Combustibles: Remove all mulch, woody shrubs, and debris within 5 feet of the church walls.
  • Hardscaping: Replace vegetation in this zone with gravel, stone, or concrete to prevent fire from touching the building.
  • Roof & Gutters: Ensure roof valleys and gutters are cleared of pine needles and leaves, which are the primary ignition points for embers.

The 5–30 Foot “Lean & Clean Zone”

  • Minimize Vegetation: Keep this area well-irrigated and free of dead wood.
  • Tree Grooming: Trim trees so that no branches are lower than 15 feet from the ground to prevent “ladder fires” from reaching the canopy.

The 30–100 Foot “Reduced Fuel Zone”

  • Thinning: Remove the most flammable vegetation (such as junipers or dry brush) and create “breaks” between clusters of trees to slow a fire’s spread.

3. Smoke Resilience & Clean Air Zones

During regional fires, the church can serve as a “Clean Air Center” for those with respiratory issues or those without air conditioning.

  • HVAC Filtration: Upgrade your system to handle MERV 13 or HEPA filters. These are high-density filters capable of scrubbing fine smoke particles (PM2.5) from the air.
  • Portable Air Scrubbers: Purchase industrial-grade portable air purifiers. These can be deployed in the “Fellowship Hall” or Sanctuary to create a safe breathing environment even if the main HVAC is limited.
  • DIY Air Scrubbing: There are also plans for DIY air purifiers made from a box fan, MERV 13 filters, and duct tape that are surprisingly effective.
  • Monitoring: Keep a digital AQI (Air Quality Index) monitor on-site to verify that the indoor air remains safe when outdoor conditions are hazardous.

4. Annual Maintenance & Readiness

Consistency is key to fire safety. Add these tasks to your church’s seasonal calendar.

  • Vegetation Management: Trim back spring growth and clear brush near buildings every year before the dry season.
  • Gutter Maintenance: Clean gutters frequently or install fire-rated gutter covers to prevent debris accumulation.
  • Evacuation Drills: Conduct an annual drill to ensure staff and volunteers know how to clear the building quickly and safely.
  • Fire Department Audit: Invite your local fire department to conduct a professional wildfire risk audit.
